# Wes Kao — framework library

The complete toolkit. Every framework Wes teaches, in her own terms, with the 🚫/✅ contrasts she uses. This is the knowledge base the agent reasons from — for both *coaching* (advice) and *rewriting* (fixing drafts). Cite the framework by name when you apply it.

---

## 1. Sales, then logistics  ⭐ (her #1 fix)

Most messages are a mix of **sales** (get buy-in — *why this matters*) and **logistics** (the *how* — steps, details). The #1 mistake: jumping to logistics before the reader is sold.

- "No one cares about how a process works… if they haven't decided to take action yet."
- A logistics-first note **assumes buy-in you don't have.** "It's easy to overestimate how much your recipient wants to do what you're asking."
- Even a rushed executive needs **30–60 seconds of why-this-matters-now** before the how.
- If you treat a sales note like a logistics note, people won't act — and you'll wrongly "fix" it by adding *more* logistics. Wrong problem.

**"Just share the why" is too simplistic.** Not all whys are equal. Pick the why most likely to compel *this* recipient — appeal to their self-interest, but not on-the-nose:
- 🚫 "This will make you look good in front of the CEO."
- ✅ "This is a chance to bring more visibility to your team's hard work."

**Reframe so the ask is a gift to them, not a favor to you.** Use FOMO ("you" want to be included), confident forward motion ("we're going to start X"), and "you-focused" not "me-focused" language. Drop the over-thanking that signals you're begging.

## 2. Being concise = density, not brevity  ⭐

- **"Being concise is not about absolute word count. It's about economy of words and density of the insight."** A 1,500-word memo can be concise; a 150-word one can be fluff.
- **The real bottleneck is clear thinking.** BLUF / "don't bury the lede" / "cut to the chase" all silently require you to *first know your main point.* If the writing won't come out clean, you usually haven't decided what you think yet. That's normal — write to find out.
- "Be more concise" is NOT a license to skip necessary detail or dodge a hard problem. Some ideas can't be reduced further without misrepresenting them (you can't reduce 11/32). Concise is a *means to being effective*, not an end.

**9 concision tactics:**
1. **Figure out your main point** (process → make sense → decide what matters → organize → say it). Rambling = doing these live, with equal weight on everything.
2. **State the main point in 2–3 sentences.** Not 1 (analysis paralysis), not 10 (monologue). It frames the conversation and buys you time.
3. **Don't narrate chronologically.** Punchline first; keep backstory short; skip the play-by-play.
4. **Run an always-on filter:** "Is this the shortest way to say it? Is this backstory really necessary? What am I actually trying to say — can I just say that?"
5. **Situational awareness:** leaders don't sound rushed — they breathe. Pause sooner than feels natural; watch for the listener losing interest.
6. **Don't be *too* concise.** Volunteer the info they'll obviously need; don't make them extract it piece by piece ("winning the battle, losing the war").
7. **Written: "main point above, context below."** Reader gets the point instantly, then reads as much context as they want.
8. **Verbal: signpost** (see #4).
9. **Proactively offer to elaborate** — and say on *what*, don't make them invent the questions.

## 3. Signposting — reduce cognitive load  ⭐ (highest-ROI writing habit)

Signposting = words/structure that tell the reader where you're taking them, so their brain doesn't burn cycles guessing. Adds information hierarchy *without* heavy formatting.

- **Levels:** memo (headers, white space) → paragraph (topic sentences, numbered lists) → sentence (transition words).
- **Makes long writing feel short, fast, skimmable** → reader actually finishes and acts.

**Her favorite signpost words (and why each earns its place):**
- **For example** — flags an example; reader can skip if they don't need it.
- **Because** — signals rationale and is independently persuasive (the Harvard copy-machine study). "If there's a way to restructure a sentence to use *because*, I'll usually do it."
- **First, second, third / next / finally** — visual hierarchy in prose.
- **Up until now / to date / going forward** — flags *change*, which humans are wired to notice. "Until now we X. Going forward we'll Y." (Managers care more about what's changing than what's steady.)
- **For context** — reader can skip if they have it; gives them agency.
- **At this point** — returns to the present; heightens drama.
- **And, but, therefore, so** — show the *logic*. Don't sloppily swap *but* and *therefore* — it changes the meaning and forces the reader to guess your logical flow.
- **This means** — elaborate implications/trade-offs after a key fact.
- **To be clear** — proactively kill predictable pushback.
- **As a next step** — jumps out, keeps the ball moving, highlights action items.

**Emotional signposting:** control the emotional subtext — readers shouldn't wonder "is this good or bad news?" Say "Good news: X." Rule of thumb: simpler ideas need less signposting; complex/long ones need more. Signpost verbally too ("First I'll cover X, then Y").

## 4. MOO — Most Obvious Objection  ⭐

Before you share an idea, spend a few seconds anticipating the **most obvious objection(s)** and address them up front. Cheap, fast, hugely effective.

- Anticipating questions makes you better at marketing, sales, negotiation, public speaking, teaching.
- "Some of the most compelling instant yeses I've given were when direct reports shared evidence that made the situation feel inevitably, obviously straightforward." → "Yes of course, let's do it now."
- 🚫 "My idea is X. What do you think?"
- ✅ "My idea is X. I figured you'd want to know it actually drove customers down the funnel, not just social likes — so I pulled the top 20 posts and…"

## 5. Strategy, not self-expression  ⭐ (feedback + any persuasion)

**A feedback conversation is a sales conversation.** The only prize is **behavior change. "Sweet, sweet behavior change."** Everything else you want to say, keep it.

- Before you speak: **"Is this strategy or self-expression?"**
  - **Strategy** = only what moves the person toward changing. ~10% of what you first want to say.
  - **Self-expression** = venting, the last word, proving you're right, making them feel remorse. The other 90%. Cut it.
- **Write out everything you want to say, then trim 90%.**
- **Mentally forgive them first.** Get the emotion out *before* the conversation (spouse, friend, therapist) so you bring neutral/positive energy. Defensiveness is the enemy — "don't trigger the defensiveness in the first place."
- **Pitch the new behavior in terms of *their* benefit:** more effective, works better with others, closer to their goals, a skill for every future role.
- **Let them talk.** End with "What's resonating most with you?" — they process out loud, you catch misunderstandings, and saying-it-themselves cements it (cognitive dissonance) better than you talking 20 more minutes.
- **Always be framing** — applies to customers, peers, your manager, even your spouse. "Selling someone on an idea is the most generous thing you can do."

## 6. Accurate confidence — tone & words  ⭐

Match conviction to evidence. Don't inflate a hypothesis into a fact; don't bury a strong recommendation under caveats.

- 🚫 "This will X." vs ✅ "This tends to X." — not the same claim. Speaking in false absolutes makes people discount everything you say "on a curve."
- "I think" isn't the real issue — *accuracy* is. Better than dropping "I think": be explicit → ✅ "I'm still working through this. My initial hunch is X."
- **Cost-of-being-wrong test:** low stakes → fine to sound certain (Twitter hot takes). A $50K decision riding on your input → overconfidence is irresponsible.
- **To invite dissent, model uncertainty:** 🚫 "We should do X, Y, Z. What do you think?" → ✅ "We *might* try X, Y, Z — not stuck on any. Where do you agree/disagree?"
- In feedback, nuance words land better: 🚫 "You are [trait]. This is ineffective. You must Z." → ✅ "I've noticed you tend to [behavior]. That might be less effective because [reason]. If you want [goal], you might consider Z." Both are direct; one sounds like an asshole.
- **Nuance words** (add if you over-assert): consider, might, could, suggest, seem, probably, likely, in general, "in my experience," "my initial reaction is," "a way" vs "the way," "almost always/never."
- **Broad-strokes words** (use sparingly in delicate moments): should, must, never, only, always, "this is THE reason." Not bad words — just back them with evidence.

## 7. CEDAF — delegate without lowering the bar

Delegating gets easier when you get better at *explaining*. Five steps:
- **C — Comprehension:** make sure they truly understand what needs doing.
- **E — Excitement:** make the task meaningful/motivating (sales, then logistics, again).
- **D — De-risk:** anticipate and pre-empt the ways it could go wrong.
- **A — Align:** confirm shared understanding — don't assume "got it" means got it.
- **F — Feedback:** create the **shortest possible feedback loop** so you correct course early.

Corollary: **volunteer the information they'll need** without being asked. Making people extract basics piece by piece is "lazy and irresponsible."

## 8. Managing up — 15 principles (condensed)

Managing up = being a competent operator who follows through and communicates well; it raises your real and perceived value. You do it *forever* — every exec still manages up (to peers, board, co-founders).

1. **Embrace it.** Your scope is 100% of your time but maybe 10% of your manager's. Take the reins proactively.
2. **Focus on the punchline.** Minimum viable backstory; don't speak in stream of consciousness.
3. **Show your thought process** — rationale, assumptions, conviction level. Even disagreement needs something to push on.
4. **Flag potential issues** early — at the first itch, not when it's a red flag. "This is a problem. Here's why it matters. I suggest X."
5. **Bring solutions, not complaints.** "This sucks, so here's what I'm thinking we can do…"
6. **Use information hierarchy** — recommendation/ask on top, context below.
7. **Keep your manager in the loop:** say what you'll do → do it → say you're doing it → say you did it. Bad news travels at your first itch.
8. **Micromanaged, or communicating badly?** Often it's the latter. Build trust and the oversight recedes.
9. **Over-communication is often the right amount** (task-relevant maturity — Andy Grove). New task → more updates; proven → fewer.
10. **Proactively assert next steps.** Don't ask "what should I do?" → "I recommend X. Rationale, risk Y, mitigation Z. If you agree I'll start with [first step]."
11. **Don't only ask questions — share a POV.** "Why x5" doesn't part the skies with smart people. Form a view on imperfect info.
12. **Anticipate questions** and answer them before they're asked (one less round-trip). Pull the number; it's worth the 10 minutes.
13. **Know when to get out** — none of this works under a manager who doesn't have your interests at heart.
14. **Be explicit about what you need:** directional feedback vs line edits? a thought partner vs a decision? a 3pm approval? Say so.
15. **Expect to manage up forever.**

## 9. Writing / editing micro-tactics (the rewrite toolkit)

Pulled from across her work — apply these line by line when fixing a draft:
- **Punchline / ask first**, context below. Number your questions so they can't be half-answered.
- **You-focused, not me-focused.** Recast "I'd like to get input" → the reader's benefit.
- **Confident forward motion** beats tentative. "We're going to start X" > "I'd like to maybe try X." Insecure vibes are a self-fulfilling prophecy.
- **Cut the over-thanking / favor-begging.** Work requests are not favors.
- **Hot keywords that double as emotional signposts:** boost morale, visibility, shine a light, small wins, in the loop. Skimmable *and* desirable.
- **Avoid the conditional "if you want to"** when you can assume they want in (FOMO).
- **Logic words must be exact** — but vs therefore vs so. Don't make the reader reconstruct your reasoning.
- **Read your writing in a robot voice** to catch where tone is ambiguous; control the emotional subtext.
- **Start right before you get eaten by the bear** — open at the moment of tension, not three paragraphs of setup.
- **Don't incept negative ideas** — don't plant doubts/objections that weren't there ("this probably won't work, but…").

## 10. Becoming a better communicator (the meta-advice)

- **Keep a swipe file.** Collect examples of communication that worked on *you* (a great cold email, a crisp memo, a line that made you say yes). The act of noticing trains pattern recognition. (See `swipe-file.md`.) Apple Notes is fine.
- **Books she recommends:** *On Writing Well* (Zinsser), *Stein on Writing*, *On Writing* (Stephen King), *Several Short Sentences About Writing* (Klinkenborg), *High Output Management* (Grove), *Your Brain at Work* (Rock).
- **Drill out loud:** say the same thing in progressively more concise versions; see how far you can push it. (How she coaches 1:1 — interrupt, compress, repeat.)
- **Invest up front:** a few extra minutes crafting the message saves hours of back-and-forth. "A little more up-front investment reaps a lot of benefits down the line."
- **Use AI as a thinking/editing partner** — she's pro-AI for drafting and pressure-testing (Claude, ChatGPT). The thinking is still yours.
- **Compounding > heroics.** "All the 'big things,' everyone else is already doing. There's not a lot of alpha in that." The edge is in the small daily reps.
